Hyperion Industries

role: Media Director
tools: InDesign, Illustrator, Premiere, Lightroom
duration: September 2016 - June 2018



In my senior year of high school, I served as the media director of a mock engineering firm known as Hyperion Industries. Our task was to build a robot known was Shere Khan that would have the ability to run, jump, climb, and attach modules to itself. As my first larger-scale design project, I had a lot to learn.

My Role

As media director, my responsibilities were very diverse and included branding, graphic design, document design, video editing, and photography. My role in crafting the company's presentation was critical; a few times throughout the school year, we had to propose our solution to "stake-holders" who would determine which proposal was better. Throughout the course of the school-year, I delivered brochures, Powerpoints, ID cards, video ads, water bottle labels, 8-foot high banners, designs for two 100-page documents, and a documentary.

Branding

Since the company was new, I first had to create the company brand. I informally surveyed members of the company asking them, "What do you want people to think when they hear our company?" Most responses included:

  • High-tech
  • Innovative
  • Modern
  • Bold

and other adjectives along those lines. With this information, I researched logos and design guidelines of companies such as Boeing, Northrop-Grumman, and Tesla, companies that team members associated with the adjectives above (we had a lot of future engineers). We were required to use red as our primary color but I knew that it was a powerful color. Using it to its strength, I tried to take a simple yet effective approach and reflect our boldness and drive for innovation in our logo. Along with this, I created a mini style guide for myself, since I was the only designer on the team.



Designing for Stakeholders

One major of my role was to prepare documents known as the System Performance Specifications (SPS) and the Proof of Concept (PoC). While I did not have to write about the technical parts of our robot proposal, I was responsible for ensuring that its presentation was professional and attractive. It was a challenge to design a document that was presentable but still communicated a high volume of technical information.

Because our team was under extremely tight deadlines, both times I was not given the text for the documents until there was less than 24 hours before the presentation. To be as efficient as possible, I first prepared the documents in InDesign and filled them with stock photos and filler text. Even then, I still had to spent a good part of my nights proofreading, making sure all images were 300 DPI, checking that all the bleeds were correct, and making any changes to the design.

With my limited time, for each document, I prioritized readability and flow since that was their main purpose. I kept each page relatively simple but ensuring that the typographic hierarchy made sense and that paragraphs were divided into logical parts.



Putting Together a Trade Show Booth

Another one of my responsibilities was to create a trade show booth to publicly present our robot near the end of the school year. Before I could actually do anything, I saved pictures of trade show booths I liked into a folder for inspiration. Once I knew which classroom we were going to be using, I first took measurements of the room and created a very basic layout of the room.



Then, I contacted different sources for trade show booth materials and determined a rough estimate of how much it would cost for different materials. Looking at our budget, we were only able to purchase massive plastic posters, instead of cloth or fabric. After figuring out the best way to divide the budget for posters on each wall, I was finally able to design the posters with proper dimensions.




Takeaways

As media director, I learned to be a jack of all trades. I had to learn new skills and to use new tools to accomplish the level of polish that I wanted in my deliverables. I learned more about designing for print, designing with purpose, doing process-driven design, and working as a solo designer with a large team. During crunch times, (of which there were many) I was incredibly stressed out by the sheer amount of work that I had to do in such a little amount of time.

Nevertheless, each successful deadline strengthened my ability to keep calm and focus on what needed to be done. With such a relatively large team compared to any past experience, I also learned how to communicate my design decisions and manage input from the rest of the team. Serving as the media director of Hyperion Industries was a demanding, yet rewarding experience.
